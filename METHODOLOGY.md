# Methodology: Post-Hoc Cumulative Budget Constraints via Optimal Execution Theory

## 1. Problem Setting

### 1.1 Wind Farm Control as an MDP

We consider a wind farm with $N$ turbines controlled by a centralized agent. At each timestep $t$, the agent observes wind conditions and turbine states $s_t$ and selects per-turbine yaw angles $a_t = (a_t^1, \ldots, a_t^N) \in [-\bar{a}, \bar{a}]^N$, where $\bar{a}$ is the maximum yaw angle (typically 30 degrees). The environment returns a reward $r_t$ proportional to total farm power production.

The standard objective is to maximize expected cumulative return:

$$\max_\pi \; \mathbb{E}_\pi \left[ \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} r(s_t, a_t) \right]$$

### 1.2 The Cumulative Budget Constraint

Negative yaw angles (steering the rotor against the conventional direction) can increase farm power through wake deflection but impose structural loads on the yawed turbine. Rather than banning negative yaw entirely, operators may specify a cumulative time budget: each turbine may operate at negative yaw for at most $B$ timesteps over a planning horizon of $T$ timesteps.

Define the cost indicator for turbine $i$:

$$c^i(a_t) = \mathbf{1}[a_t^i < -\theta]$$

where $\theta \geq 0$ is a configurable threshold (default 0). The cumulative budget constraint is:

$$\sum_{t=0}^{T-1} c^i(a_t) \leq B \quad \forall \, i \in \{1, \ldots, N\}$$

This produces a Constrained MDP (CMDP):

$$\max_\pi \; \mathbb{E}_\pi \left[ \sum_t r(s_t, a_t) \right] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \mathbb{E}_\pi \left[ \sum_t c^i(a_t) \right] \leq B \quad \forall \, i$$

### 1.3 The Post-Hoc Composition Requirement

Standard CMDP approaches (Lagrangian relaxation, constrained policy optimization) require retraining the policy for each constraint specification $(B, \theta)$. We instead seek a method that:

1. Trains the policy **once**, unconstrained
2. Composes the budget constraint at **inference time** without retraining
3. Adapts instantly when the budget changes (e.g., after a turbine bearing replacement)

This is possible because our actor architecture (Energy-Based Transformer) supports compositional energy functions.

## 2. Energy-Based Transformer Actor

### 2.1 Architecture

The EBT actor treats each turbine as a token in a transformer encoder. Given observations $s_t$, the encoder produces per-turbine embeddings $\mathbf{e}_t^i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ for $i = 1, \ldots, N$. An energy head $E_\phi$ maps embeddings and candidate actions to per-turbine scalar energies:

$$E_\phi^i(\mathbf{e}_t^i, a^i) \in \mathbb{R} \quad \text{(lower energy = better action)}$$

### 2.2 Action Generation via Gradient Descent

Actions are generated by minimizing the energy landscape through gradient descent. Starting from random candidates $a^{(0)} \sim \mathcal{U}[-1, 1]^N$:

$$a^{(k+1)} = \text{clip}\left(a^{(k)} - \eta \nabla_a E_\text{total}(a^{(k)}), \; -1, \; 1\right)$$

for $k = 0, \ldots, K-1$ optimization steps, where $\eta$ is the step size.

### 2.3 Per-Turbine Energy Composition

The key property enabling post-hoc constraints: external energy terms compose with the actor energy **per-turbine, before aggregation**:

$$E_\text{total}(a) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ E_\phi^i(\mathbf{e}_t^i, a^i) + \lambda_\text{gs} \cdot E_\text{constraint}^i(a^i, t) \right]$$

where $\lambda_\text{gs}$ is the guidance scale (overall constraint strength). Because composition happens before the mean over turbines, each turbine receives its own constraint gradient, enabling heterogeneous per-turbine constraints.

## 3. Almgren-Chriss-Inspired Budget Schedule

### 3.1 Analogy to Optimal Execution

The budget allocation problem is structurally analogous to optimal trade execution in quantitative finance:

| Optimal Execution | Wind Farm Budget |
|---|---|
| Shares to liquidate | Negative-yaw timestep budget $B$ |
| Trading horizon | Planning horizon $T$ |
| Market conditions | Wind speed and direction |
| Execution rate | Rate of budget consumption |
| Risk aversion $\eta$ | Concentration vs. uniform spending |
| TWAP baseline | Uniform budget spending |

In the Almgren-Chriss framework, a risk-averse trader balances the cost of immediacy against the risk of future price uncertainty. Analogously, a budget-constrained wind farm operator balances the benefit of using negative yaw now against the risk that better opportunities arise later.

### 3.2 Time-Varying Penalty Weight

We define a time-varying penalty weight $\lambda(t)$ that modulates the negative-yaw constraint based on remaining budget and remaining time. This is inspired by (but not identical to) the Almgren-Chriss optimal liquidation trajectory.

**Budget tracking.** At each timestep, we track the cumulative negative-yaw count per turbine:

$$C_t^i = \sum_{s=0}^{t-1} c^i(a_s)$$

**Urgency ratio.** Define the budget fraction and time fraction remaining:

$$\beta_t^i = \frac{B - C_t^i}{B}, \qquad \tau_t = \frac{T - t}{T}$$

The urgency ratio measures whether the turbine is ahead of or behind the uniform (TWAP) spending schedule:

$$u_t^i = \frac{\beta_t^i}{\tau_t}$$

When $u_t^i > 1$, the turbine has spent less budget than the TWAP trajectory expects (surplus). When $u_t^i < 1$, it has overspent (deficit).

**AC-inspired weight.** The penalty weight is:

$$\lambda_\text{AC}^i(t) = \exp\!\left(\eta \cdot \left(\frac{1}{u_t^i} - 1\right)\right)$$

where $\eta \geq 0$ is the risk aversion parameter. Properties:

- $u_t^i = 1 \implies \lambda = 1$ (on TWAP trajectory: baseline penalty)
- $u_t^i > 1 \implies \lambda < 1$ (budget surplus: relax penalty, spend freely)
- $u_t^i < 1 \implies \lambda > 1$ (budget deficit: tighten penalty, conserve)
- $\eta = 0 \implies \lambda = 1 \; \forall t$ (TWAP recovery: constant penalty regardless of budget state)

**Hard wall backstop.** To prevent budget exhaustion, a steep exponential wall activates when budget drops below 5%:

$$\lambda_\text{wall}^i(t) = \exp\!\left(k \cdot \text{ReLU}\!\left(1 - \frac{\beta_t^i}{0.05}\right)\right)$$

where $k$ is the wall steepness (default 10). The combined weight is:

$$\lambda^i(t) = \min\!\left(\lambda_\text{AC}^i(t) \cdot \lambda_\text{wall}^i(t), \; \lambda_\text{max}\right)$$

clamped to $\lambda_\text{max} = 10^6$ to prevent infinite gradients.

### 3.3 Per-Turbine Constraint Energy

The constraint energy for turbine $i$ at time $t$ is:

$$E_\text{budget}^i(a^i, t) = \lambda^i(t) \cdot \left(\exp\!\left(k \cdot \text{ReLU}(-a^i - \theta)\right) - 1\right)$$

The base penalty $\exp(k \cdot \text{ReLU}(-a^i - \theta)) - 1$ is zero for positive yaw ($a^i > -\theta$) and grows exponentially for negative yaw, following the same functional form used by the existing yaw-limit surrogates in this codebase. The time-varying weight $\lambda^i(t)$ modulates the penalty strength based on remaining budget.

### 3.4 Composed Energy Landscape

During inference, the total energy optimized by the EBT actor becomes:

$$E_\text{total}(a, t) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ E_\phi^i(\mathbf{e}_t^i, a^i) + \lambda_\text{gs} \cdot \lambda^i(t) \cdot \left(\exp\!\left(k \cdot \text{ReLU}(-a^i - \theta)\right) - 1\right) \right]$$

Gradient descent on this landscape simultaneously optimizes power (through $E_\phi$) and respects the budget (through the time-varying penalty). No retraining is needed: $E_\phi$ is frozen from the unconstrained training, and the budget term is added post-hoc.

### 3.5 Diffusion Actor Variant

For the diffusion actor, the same constraint applies via classifier guidance during DDIM denoising. At each denoising step $n$:

$$x_{t}^{(n)} \leftarrow x_{t}^{(n)} - \lambda_\text{gs} \cdot \nabla_{x} E_\text{budget}^{\text{scalar}}(x_t^{(n)}, t)$$

where $E_\text{budget}^{\text{scalar}} = \sum_i E_\text{budget}^i$ is the scalar sum over turbines.

## 4. Algorithm

The full inference-time algorithm for one episode:

```
Input: Trained EBT actor E_phi, budget B, horizon T,
       risk aversion eta, guidance scale lambda_gs, steepness k
Initialize: C^i = 0 for all turbines i

For t = 0, ..., T-1:
    1. Observe wind state s_t
    2. Compute per-turbine lambda^i(t) from (C^i, B, T-t, eta, k)
    3. Generate actions via gradient descent on composed energy:
         a_t = argmin_a  (1/N) sum_i [E_phi^i(e^i, a^i)
                + lambda_gs * lambda^i(t) * (exp(k * ReLU(-a^i - theta)) - 1)]
    4. Execute a_t in environment, observe r_t
    5. Update budget tracking: C^i += 1[a_t^i < -theta]
```

The only additions to the standard EBT inference loop are steps 2 and 5: computing the time-varying weight (a few floating-point operations) and updating the budget counter (one comparison per turbine). The computational overhead is negligible.

## 5. Heterogeneous Per-Turbine Budgets

The framework naturally supports per-turbine budgets $B^i$ by replacing the uniform $B$ in the urgency calculation:

$$\beta_t^i = \frac{B^i - C_t^i}{B^i}, \qquad u_t^i = \frac{\beta_t^i}{\tau_t}$$

This enables scenarios where turbines have different structural health states (e.g., turbine 3 recently had a bearing replacement and can tolerate more negative yaw).

## 6. Connection to CMDP Lagrangian Duality

The standard Lagrangian relaxation of the CMDP introduces a dual variable $\mu$ for the budget constraint:

$$\mathcal{L}(\pi, \mu) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_t r(s_t, a_t)\right] - \mu \cdot \left(\mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_t c(a_t)\right] - B\right)$$

In standard approaches, $\mu$ is learned via dual gradient ascent:

$$\mu_{k+1} = \max\left(0, \; \mu_k + \alpha \cdot \left(\hat{C}_k - B\right)\right)$$

which requires iterating between policy optimization (primal) and multiplier updates (dual) — i.e., retraining.

Our time-varying weight $\lambda^i(t)$ serves a similar functional role to $\mu$ but is computed in closed form from the current budget state $(C_t^i, B^i, T-t)$ without any optimization. It shares key qualitative properties with the dual variable:

- **Tightens when overspending** ($C_t^i$ large relative to $t/T$): both $\mu$ and $\lambda$ increase
- **Relaxes when underspending**: both decrease
- **Converges to a boundary regime** as budget depletes: $\mu$ grows via gradient ascent, $\lambda$ grows via the hard wall

The crucial difference: $\mu$ requires retraining the policy each time it updates, while $\lambda(t)$ composes post-hoc with the frozen policy. This enables instant adaptation to new budget specifications.

## 7. Parameters

| Symbol | Name | Default | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| $B$ | Budget steps | 15 | Total allowed negative-yaw timesteps per turbine |
| $T$ | Horizon steps | 200 | Episode/planning horizon length |
| $\eta$ | Risk aversion | 2.0 | Concentration vs. uniform spending (0 = TWAP) |
| $\lambda_\text{gs}$ | Guidance scale | 1.0 | Overall constraint strength |
| $k$ | Steepness | 10.0 | Exponential wall steepness |
| $\theta$ | Neg-yaw threshold | 0.0 | Yaw angles below $-\theta$ counted as negative |
| $\lambda_\text{max}$ | Max weight | $10^6$ | Clamp to prevent infinite gradients |

## 8. Empirical Properties

The following properties have been verified numerically (see `ac_theory.py`):

1. **TWAP recovery**: $\eta = 0 \implies \lambda^i(t) = 1$ for all $t$ and $i$ (analytically exact, since $\exp(0) = 1$).

2. **Monotonic tightening under overspending**: When a turbine consistently uses negative yaw, $\lambda^i(t)$ increases monotonically until the hard wall activates. Verified: $\lambda^i(t+1) \geq \lambda^i(t)$ when $c^i(a_t) = 1$ for all tested trajectories.

3. **Budget satisfaction**: With the hard guard ($C_t^i < B$ required for spending), the budget is never exceeded. Verified: 0 violations across 3000 random trials with 6 risk aversion values.

4. **Regret scaling**: Under i.i.d. benefits, the gap between oracle (hindsight top-$B$ selection) and the AC schedule scales approximately as $O(\sqrt{T})$. Verified empirically: regret$/\sqrt{T}$ ratio is approximately constant (CV = 0.215) across horizons $T \in \{50, 100, 200, 500, 1000\}$. Note: this is an empirical observation, not a proven bound.

## 9. Experimental Validation

### 9.1 Closed-Loop RL Evaluation

A 3-turbine EBT-SAC agent was trained unconstrained for 100,000 steps in WindGym (PyWake backend). At inference, the `NegativeYawBudgetSurrogate` was composed post-hoc with budget $B=15$ steps over a $T=200$ step episode.

**Key result**: The unconstrained agent uses negative yaw on 200/200 steps for T0 and 198/200 for T1. With the budget surrogate, all 25 tested (RA, guidance_scale) configurations respect the 15-step budget. The maximum observed negative-yaw count was 11 steps per turbine (RA=5.0, gs=0.5), well within the budget.

### 9.2 Open-Loop Budget Allocation (Energy Island Wind Data)

Using 10 years of daily wind measurements from Energy Island (Denmark), the AC schedule was compared to baselines on a budget allocation problem:

| Method | % of Oracle (avg over 10 years) |
|---|---|
| Oracle (hindsight) | 100% |
| AC ($\eta = 2$) | 85% |
| Greedy (myopic) | 65% |
| TWAP (uniform) | 22% |

The AC schedule captures 85% of the hindsight-optimal value by concentrating budget spending into high-benefit wind conditions, compared to 65% for greedy (which exhausts budget on the first good days).

### 9.3 Second Domain: Battery Cycle-Life Budget

The same framework was applied to a grid battery storage problem where deep-discharge cycles are limited per year. AC captured 83.5% of oracle value vs. 73.8% for greedy, demonstrating the mechanism generalizes beyond wind.
