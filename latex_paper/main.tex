\begin{abstract}
Deploying reinforcement learning policies in the real world often requires cumulative operational constraints unknown at training time---maintenance budgets, emission limits, duty-cycle restrictions.
Standard constrained MDP approaches require full retraining for each constraint specification, making them impractical when constraints change post-deployment.
We propose a closed-form, time-varying penalty weight that adapts constraint enforcement to budget state: the \emph{urgency ratio} $u$ (remaining budget fraction divided by remaining time fraction) drives a weight $w(u) = \exp(\eta(1/u - 1))$ with a single risk-aversion parameter $\eta$.
The schedule composes post-hoc with pre-trained policies---via per-agent energy addition for energy-based actors or gradient-based action correction for standard actors---requiring zero retraining.
A single pre-trained policy thereby supports arbitrary budget levels, per-agent heterogeneous constraints, and instant adaptation to new specifications.
When paired with a differentiable nonlinear cost function, the method captures physically meaningful constraint structure: on a wind farm yaw-fatigue problem, a nonlinear fatigue surrogate achieves a $6.8\times$ reduction in cumulative structural fatigue compared to a binary cost indicator, while retaining 98\% of unconstrained power.
We validate on wind farm yaw control, demonstrating budget satisfaction with high reward retention, and use Safety Gymnasium navigation as a diagnostic for state-dependent costs where the method partially succeeds (20--36\% cost reduction via a learned cost critic) but cannot satisfy tight budgets due to weak action-to-cost coupling.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Consider a wind farm operator who trains a reinforcement learning (RL) controller to maximize power production.
Six months after deployment, a bearing inspection reveals that one turbine needs restricted yaw travel: it may operate at negative yaw angles for at most 15 hours per year to limit structural fatigue.
Retraining the controller is expensive and disruptive.
The operator needs a way to impose this new cumulative constraint on the \emph{existing} policy, instantly and without retraining.

This constraint has a subtle structure.
Simple fatigue models treat yaw angle as an instantaneous cost---the load depends only on the current action.
But realistic structural loading depends on \emph{partial wake interactions}: an upstream turbine's yaw deflects its wake, which propagates downstream over seconds to minutes, creating asymmetric inflow that drives fatigue on downstream rotors~\citep{dijk2025load}.
The cost at one turbine is caused by another turbine's action in the past, mediated by wake dynamics---a \emph{state-dependent} cost that cannot be controlled by modifying the current action alone.
This delayed, state-mediated cost structure is not unique to wind farms: it arises whenever cumulative constraints depend on propagation dynamics (thermal systems, chemical processes, multi-agent interactions).

More broadly, cumulative operational constraints arise whenever an agent must limit total time in a high-reward but costly regime:
wind turbines with load budgets,
robots with joint-wear maintenance cycles,
grid batteries with cycle-life limits,
and advertisers with daily spending caps~\citep{balseiro2023budget}.
Unlike instantaneous constraints, these are \emph{lifetime budgets}: the agent must decide \emph{when} to spend its limited capacity for risky actions.

This budget allocation problem shares a conceptual structure with optimal trade execution in quantitative finance~\citep{almgren2001optimal}: a trader must liquidate a position over a time horizon, deciding when to trade aggressively versus passively.
While the full financial model involves market impact dynamics with no analog in our setting, a key insight transfers: the rate of resource consumption should depend on \emph{remaining inventory relative to remaining time}---the urgency ratio.

Existing approaches to constrained RL fall into two categories, neither of which solves this problem:
(1)~\textbf{Constrained MDP methods}~\citep{altman1999constrained, stooke2020responsive, sootla2022saute, chen2025predictive} learn a Lagrangian dual variable during training, requiring full retraining for each new constraint specification.
(2)~\textbf{Post-hoc constraint composition}~\citep{urain2023composable, zhang2025constrained, xiao2023safediffuser} adds constraints at inference time via energy composition or classifier guidance, but handles only \emph{instantaneous} constraints (barrier functions, geometric limits)---not cumulative budgets that span an episode.

We fill this gap.
Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{closed-form time-varying penalty weight} motivated by budget-balanced sequential allocation under an idealized Boltzmann response (Section~\ref{sec:theory}). The analysis identifies the urgency ratio $u$ as the natural state variable and constrains the schedule to be monotone decreasing in $u$.
    \item A \textbf{practical schedule} $w(u) = \exp(\eta(1/u - 1))$ that recovers uniform allocation at $\eta=0$ and provides tunable risk aversion via a single scalar parameter (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
    \item \textbf{Post-hoc composition} with pre-trained policies---via per-agent energy addition for EBT actors or gradient-based action correction for standard actors---enabling zero-retraining constraint enforcement with per-agent budgets (Section~\ref{sec:composition}).
    \item \textbf{Empirical validation} across two domains---wind farm yaw control with structural fatigue budgets and Safety Gymnasium navigation with hazard-exposure budgets---showing budget satisfaction, reward retention, and a $6.8\times$ fatigue reduction when using nonlinear cost surrogates (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Constrained MDPs and Lagrangian Relaxation}

A Constrained MDP (CMDP)~\citep{altman1999constrained} augments the standard MDP objective with cost constraints:
\begin{equation}
    \max_\pi \; \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T-1} r(s_t, a_t)\right]
    \quad \text{s.t.} \quad
    \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T-1} c^i(s_t, a_t)\right] \leq B^i
    \quad \forall\, i
    \label{eq:cmdp}
\end{equation}
where $c^i$ is a per-agent cost indicator and $B^i$ is the lifetime budget for agent $i$.
The standard approach introduces a Lagrangian dual variable $\mu$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\pi, \mu) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_t r(s_t, a_t)\right] - \mu \cdot \left(\mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_t c(s_t, a_t)\right] - B\right)
\end{equation}
and alternates between policy optimization (primal) and multiplier updates via dual gradient ascent: $\mu_{k+1} = \max(0,\, \mu_k + \alpha(\hat{C}_k - B))$~\citep{stooke2020responsive}.
This requires retraining the policy whenever $B$ changes.

\subsection{Energy-Based RL and Post-Hoc Composition}

Energy-Based Transformer (EBT) actors~\citep{gladstone2025ebt} generate actions by gradient descent on a learned energy landscape.
Given per-agent embeddings $\mathbf{e}^i$ from a transformer encoder, an energy head $E_\phi^i(\mathbf{e}^i, a^i)$ assigns scalar energy to candidate actions.
Actions are optimized via:
\begin{equation}
    a^{(k+1)} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(a^{(k)} - \eta_{\mathrm{lr}} \nabla_a E_{\mathrm{total}}(a^{(k)}),\; -1,\; 1\right)
\end{equation}
The key property enabling post-hoc constraints: external energy terms compose \emph{per-agent, before aggregation}:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\mathrm{total}}(a) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\!\left[E_\phi^i(\mathbf{e}^i, a^i) + \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot E_{\mathrm{constraint}}^i(a^i)\right]
    \label{eq:composition}
\end{equation}
This enables adding new constraints at inference time without modifying $E_\phi$.
Prior work composes instantaneous constraints (\eg, yaw limits, obstacle avoidance)~\citep{urain2023composable, zhang2025constrained}.
We extend this to \emph{cumulative budget constraints} via a time-varying energy weight.

\section{Optimal Budget Scheduling}
\label{sec:theory}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We motivate the penalty weight schedule from a sequential budget allocation problem.
The derivation identifies the urgency ratio as the natural state variable and constrains the design space; the specific functional form is a principled choice justified by its analytical properties rather than exact optimality for any particular policy class.

\textbf{Setup.}
An agent must allocate $B$ ``risky'' actions over $T$ time steps.
At each step $t$, the agent observes conditions and decides whether to spend one unit of budget.
Spending yields a time-dependent benefit; the goal is to maximize total benefit subject to the budget constraint.

\textbf{Budget tracking.}
Let $C_t^i = \sum_{s=0}^{t-1} c^i(a_s)$ denote the cumulative cost for agent $i$ at time $t$, where $c^i(a) = \mathbf{1}[a^i \in \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{risky}}]$ is the cost indicator (\eg, $\mathbf{1}[\text{yaw} < 0]$ for wind farms, $\mathbf{1}[\text{cost} > 0]$ for hazard proximity in safe navigation).
Define the \emph{urgency ratio}:
\begin{equation}
    u_t^i = \frac{\beta_t^i}{\tau_t}, \qquad
    \beta_t^i = \frac{B^i - C_t^i}{B^i}, \qquad
    \tau_t = \frac{T - t}{T}
    \label{eq:urgency}
\end{equation}
where $\beta_t^i$ is the remaining budget fraction and $\tau_t$ is the remaining time fraction.
When $u > 1$, the agent has spent less than the uniform (TWAP) rate; when $u < 1$, it has overspent.

\begin{proposition}[Motivating analysis under idealized Boltzmann response]
\label{prop:optimal}
Consider an idealized policy that responds to penalty weight $\lambda$ via a Boltzmann model:
$P(\text{risky} \mid \lambda) = P_0 \exp(-\alpha \lambda)$,
where $P_0$ is the base risky-action probability and $\alpha > 0$ is the policy's sensitivity.
Under this model, the penalty weight that maintains budget balance has a multiplicative structure determined by the urgency ratio:
\begin{equation}
    w^*(u) = \frac{1}{u}
    \label{eq:optimal}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Budget balance requires that the expected number of risky actions over the remaining horizon equals the remaining budget:
\begin{equation}
    (T - t) \cdot P(\text{risky} \mid \lambda) = B^i - C_t^i
    \label{eq:balance}
\end{equation}
Substituting the Boltzmann model: $(T-t) \cdot P_0 \exp(-\alpha \lambda) = B^i - C_t^i$.
Solving: $\lambda = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\!\frac{(T-t)\,P_0}{B^i - C_t^i}$.
At the TWAP baseline ($u = 1$, where $\beta = \tau$, \ie, $(B^i - C_t^i)/B^i = (T-t)/T$), the budget balance gives $\lambda_0 = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\!\frac{T P_0}{B^i}$.
The multiplicative weight relative to the TWAP penalty is:
\[
    w = \exp\!\big(\alpha(\lambda - \lambda_0)\big) = \frac{(T-t)\,P_0}{B^i - C_t^i} \cdot \frac{B^i}{T\,P_0} = \frac{(T-t)/T}{(B^i - C_t^i)/B^i} = \frac{\tau}{\beta} = \frac{1}{u}
\]
\end{proof}

\textbf{Practical schedule.}
Since $1/u \to \infty$ as $u \to 0$ (budget depleted) and the Boltzmann response is an idealization that does not hold for real policy architectures, we use a parametric family that preserves the key structural properties:
\begin{equation}
    w(u) = \exp\!\left(\eta \cdot \left(\frac{1}{u} - 1\right)\right)
    \label{eq:practical}
\end{equation}

The parameter $\eta \geq 0$ controls risk aversion:
$\eta = 0$ recovers the constant-penalty baseline (no adaptation to budget state);
$\eta > 0$ increases the penalty when overspent ($u < 1$) and relaxes it when underspent ($u > 1$).

\textbf{Hard wall backstop.}
A steep exponential barrier activates when budget drops below 5\%:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{\mathrm{wall}}^i(t) = \exp\!\left(k \cdot \mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(1 - \frac{\beta_t^i}{0.05}\right)\right)
\end{equation}
The combined weight is $\lambda^i(t) = \min(w(u_t^i) \cdot \lambda_{\mathrm{wall}}^i(t),\; \lambda_{\max})$, clamped at $\lambda_{\max} = 10^6$.

\textbf{Approximation quality and defense in depth.}
The practical schedule $\exp(\eta(1/u-1))$ matches $u^{-\eta}$ within 5\% for $u \in [0.7, 1.3]$ but diverges for $u < 0.3$ (Figure~\ref{fig:approx}).
This is by design: the method operates in three regimes.
In the \emph{normal} regime ($u \in [0.5, 1.5]$), the smooth schedule paces spending.
In the \emph{depletion} regime ($u < 0.05$), the hard wall backstop provides a steep barrier.
Between them, both the smooth schedule and the wall contribute.
The hard guard ($C^i < B^i$) provides an absolute guarantee.
Ablation results (Table~\ref{tab:hard_guard}) show that the smooth schedule alone is sufficient in practice---the hard guard does not change results.

\textbf{Penalty landscape.}
Figure~\ref{fig:contours} visualizes $\lambda(\beta, \tau)$ as a function of remaining budget fraction $\beta$ and remaining time fraction $\tau$ for three risk-aversion values.
The TWAP diagonal ($\beta = \tau$, where $u=1$) separates the surplus region ($\lambda < 1$, agent may spend freely) from the deficit region ($\lambda > 1$, agent conserves).
At $\eta = 0.5$, gradients are gentle---the schedule tolerates moderate budget imbalance.
At $\eta = 5$, the penalty rises steeply near the diagonal, aggressively penalizing any deviation from the TWAP trajectory.
The hard wall (red band at $\beta < 0.05$) provides a safety backstop independent of $\eta$.

\textbf{Note on the Boltzmann assumption.}
Proposition~\ref{prop:optimal} assumes a Boltzmann response, which is approximate for EBT actors optimized by gradient descent and does not hold for standard MLP policies.
The derivation serves to identify the urgency ratio $u$ as the natural state variable and to constrain the schedule to be monotone decreasing in $u$ with TWAP recovery at $\eta=0$.
The specific functional form $\exp(\eta(1/u-1))$ is a parametric choice satisfying these properties; it is not claimed to be optimal for any particular policy architecture.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The method has three components that compose independently:
(1)~the \emph{budget schedule} $\lambda(t)$ from Section~\ref{sec:theory}, which determines \emph{how strictly} to enforce the constraint at each moment;
(2)~the \emph{cost function} $c(s, a)$, which defines \emph{what} is costly;
and (3)~the \emph{application mechanism}, which determines \emph{how} the penalty modifies actions.
Any cost function composes with any mechanism and the same $\lambda(t)$ schedule.

\subsection{Cost Functions: From Binary Indicators to Nonlinear Surrogates}
\label{sec:cost}

The simplest cost is a binary indicator: $c(a) = \mathbf{1}[a \in \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{risky}}]$ (\eg, $\mathbf{1}[\text{yaw} < 0]$).
This counts timesteps of risky behavior but ignores severity---10$^\circ$ of negative yaw is penalized the same as 30$^\circ$.

For real applications, a \emph{nonlinear surrogate} captures the physics:
\begin{equation}
    c(s, a) = \left(\frac{|a|}{\bar{a}}\right)^\beta \cdot \left(\frac{v(s)}{v_{\mathrm{ref}}}\right)^\gamma
    \label{eq:nonlinear_cost}
\end{equation}
where $v(s)$ is a state-dependent amplifier (\eg, wind speed), $\beta > 1$ captures superlinear damage from large actions, and $\gamma > 1$ captures environmental amplification.
For wind turbine fatigue, $\beta = 2$ (quadratic yaw-fatigue) and $\gamma = 3$ (cubic wind-speed amplification) are physically motivated~\citep{dijk2025load}.

The cost function must be differentiable for gradient-based application mechanisms (Section~\ref{sec:gradient}).
All cost functions compose with the same $\lambda(t)$ schedule; swapping the cost requires no retraining.

\subsection{Application Mechanism 1: Energy Composition (EBT Actors)}
\label{sec:composition}

For Energy-Based Transformer actors, the cost composes directly with the policy energy:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\mathrm{total}}(a, t) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\!\left[E_\phi^i(\mathbf{e}_t^i, a^i) + \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot \lambda^i(t) \cdot P(c(s, a^i))\right]
    \label{eq:ebt_composition}
\end{equation}
where $P(x) = \exp(k \cdot \mathrm{ReLU}(x)) - 1$ is an exponential penalty and $\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}}$ is the guidance scale.
Gradient descent on $E_{\mathrm{total}}$ simultaneously optimizes reward and respects the budget.
This is the native mechanism for EBT actors and produces the strongest results.

\subsection{Application Mechanism 2: Gradient-Based Action Correction (Any Actor)}
\label{sec:gradient}

For standard MLP or Gaussian actors that do not support energy composition, we correct the actor's output using the cost function's gradient:
\begin{equation}
    a_{\mathrm{corrected}} = a_{\mathrm{actor}} - \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot \lambda(t) \cdot \alpha \cdot \nabla_a\, c(s, a_{\mathrm{actor}})
    \label{eq:gradient_correction}
\end{equation}
iterated for $K$ steps (typically $K = 3$--$5$), where $\alpha$ is a step size.
This is mathematically equivalent to one step of gradient descent on the composed energy $E_\phi + \lambda(t) \cdot c$, but applied to the frozen actor's output rather than optimized from scratch.

The gradient naturally handles nonlinear and spatial costs that action scaling cannot.
For velocity constraints, $\nabla_a c$ points toward lower action magnitude (equivalent to action scaling).
For spatial constraints (\eg, hazard proximity), $\nabla_a c$ points \emph{away from hazards}---redirecting the agent's trajectory, not just slowing it down.

\subsection{Algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Post-Hoc Budget-Constrained Inference}
\label{alg:budget}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Trained actor, budget $B^i$ per agent, horizon $T$, risk aversion $\eta$, cost function $c(s, a)$
\STATE Initialize $C^i \leftarrow 0$ for all agents $i$
\FOR{$t = 0, \ldots, T-1$}
    \STATE Observe state $s_t$
    \STATE Compute urgency $u_t^i = \frac{(B^i - C^i)/B^i}{(T-t)/T}$ and $\lambda^i(t) = \min(\exp(\eta(1/u_t^i - 1)) \cdot \lambda_{\mathrm{wall}}^i,\; \lambda_{\max})$
    \STATE Generate action: \hfill\COMMENT{choose one mechanism}
    \STATE \quad \textit{EBT:} gradient descent on $E_\phi + \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot \lambda^i(t) \cdot P(c(s, a))$
    \STATE \quad \textit{MLP:} $a \leftarrow a_{\mathrm{actor}} - \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot \lambda^i(t) \cdot \alpha \cdot \nabla_a c(s, a)$ for $K$ steps
    \STATE Execute $a_t$, observe $r_t$, compute $c_t = c(s_t, a_t)$
    \STATE Update $C^i \leftarrow C^i + c_t^i$ \hfill\COMMENT{budget tracking}
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The computational overhead of the $\lambda$ computation is negligible relative to the policy forward pass (see Section~\ref{sec:results} for timing measurements).
Gradient correction adds $K$ backward passes through the cost function, which is typically much smaller than the policy network.

\textbf{Tuning requirements.}
The schedule parameter $\eta$ and the guidance scale $\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}}$ require mild calibration: $\eta$ controls how aggressively the schedule adapts, while $\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}}$ balances the constraint energy against the learned policy energy.
This calibration is substantially cheaper than retraining---it involves only evaluation episodes, not gradient updates---but is not zero-cost.
We find $\eta \in [2, 5]$ and $\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \in [0.05, 0.5]$ work well across both domains tested.

\subsection{Connection to CMDP Lagrangian Duality}

The time-varying $\lambda^i(t)$ serves a similar role to the Lagrangian dual variable $\mu$ in~\eqref{eq:cmdp}: it penalizes costly actions proportionally to budget scarcity.
The key difference: $\mu$ is learned via dual gradient ascent (requiring retraining); $\lambda^i(t)$ is computed in closed form from the current budget state, enabling instant adaptation to new specifications.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We evaluate the budget scheduling mechanism across two domains with different risky-action semantics.

\subsection{Domain 1: Wind Farm Yaw Control}

\textbf{Environment.}
WindGym~\citep{windgym} with PyWake backend.
We test on two layouts: a 3-turbine row and a 9-turbine $3\!\times\!3$ grid.
Wind speed sampled from 10--14~m/s, wind direction from 225--315$^\circ$, providing natural temporal variation in wake conditions.

\textbf{Risky action.}
Negative yaw angles ($a^i < 0$) steer the rotor against the conventional direction, deflecting wakes to increase downstream power but imposing structural loads on the yawed turbine~\citep{dijk2025load}.

\textbf{Budget.}
$B$ = total timesteps at negative yaw per turbine per episode.

\textbf{Agent.}
Energy-Based Transformer (EBT) actor with Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), trained unconstrained for 100k steps.
The budget surrogate composes post-hoc via per-turbine energy addition at inference.

\subsection{Domain 2: Safety Gymnasium Navigation}

\textbf{Environment.}
SafetyPointGoal1-v0 from Safety-Gymnasium~\citep{ji2023safetygym}: a point robot navigates to a goal position in an arena containing circular hazard zones.
The environment provides a binary cost signal ($c=1$) when the agent enters a hazard zone.

\textbf{Risky action.}
Entering hazard proximity zones (cost $> 0$).
Unlike the wind farm domain where the risky action is a specific control direction (negative yaw), here the cost is spatial---it depends on the agent's position relative to hazards, not the action direction.

\textbf{Budget.}
$B$ = total cost-incurring timesteps, tested at $B \in \{10, 25, 40, 56\}$ corresponding to approximately 18\%, 45\%, 71\%, and 100\% of unconstrained hazard exposure ($\sim$56 steps).

\textbf{Agent.}
SAC with Gaussian actor (standard MLP, 256 hidden units), trained unconstrained for 200k steps.
We test two application mechanisms: (1)~action scaling, where $a \leftarrow a / (1 + 0.1 \cdot \lambda(t) \cdot p(a))$; and (2)~gradient correction (Eq.~\ref{eq:gradient_correction}), which uses the gradient of a differentiable hazard cost proxy to steer the agent away from hazards.
This domain demonstrates that the budget schedule is architecture-agnostic and that gradient correction is necessary when the cost is spatial rather than directional.

\subsection{Baselines}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unconstrained}: no budget constraint (upper bound on reward).
    \item \textbf{Hard clip}: risky action banned entirely (lower bound on reward).
    \item \textbf{Constant penalty} ($\eta = 0$): fixed penalty weight with no adaptation to budget state, equivalent to a static Lagrangian multiplier.
    \item \textbf{AC schedule} ($\eta > 0$): our time-varying weight with risk aversion $\eta \in \{2, 5\}$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Ablations (Wind Farm)}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Hard guard vs.\ AC schedule}: Does the schedule alone prevent budget violations, or is the hard guard ($C^i < B^i$) necessary?
    \item \textbf{$1/u^{\eta}$ vs.\ $\exp(\eta(1/u-1))$}: Theoretically motivated schedule vs.\ practical approximation.
    \item \textbf{Budget flexibility}: Same policy at budget levels from 2.5\% to 50\% of episode length.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Spatial allocation.}
Figure~\ref{fig:wf_spatial}(a) reveals that the unconstrained agent has learned wake physics: it preferentially applies negative yaw to the upstream turbine (T0, 122 steps) to deflect its wake, benefiting downstream turbines.
Under the AC budget constraint ($B=15$), all three turbines are constrained to $\sim$10 steps each, while the constant penalty ($\eta=0$) over-spends at $\sim$50 steps per turbine.
Figure~\ref{fig:wf_budget}(a) shows how the agent allocates negative yaw across budget levels.
As the budget tightens, the downstream turbine (T2) reduces negative yaw first, preserving the more valuable upstream steering.
Power retention (Figure~\ref{fig:wf_budget}b) is high across all budget levels---the median constrained power is within 5\% of unconstrained, with large IQR bands reflecting the stochastic wind conditions (variable speed and direction across episodes).
The near-100\% retention indicates that the budget constraint has minimal power cost at these budget levels, consistent with the wind farm result that negative yaw provides marginal benefit at oblique wind directions.

\subsection{The Action-to-Cost Gap: From Wind Farm to Safety Gym}
\label{sec:state_dependent}

The wind farm results demonstrate that the urgency schedule works well when cost is a direct function of the current action.
But this success rests on a structural property of the simplified fatigue model: the cost $c = |y/\bar{y}|^\beta \cdot (v/v_{\mathrm{ref}})^\gamma$ depends on the current yaw angle and wind speed, both observable and controllable at decision time.
Real structural fatigue has a different causal structure.

\textbf{The wake propagation problem.}
In a real wind farm, fatigue on downstream turbines is driven by \emph{partial wake interactions}: an upstream turbine's yaw deflects its wake, which propagates downstream over 10--60 seconds (depending on spacing and wind speed), creating asymmetric inflow that drives cyclic loading on the downstream rotor.
The cost at turbine $j$ at time $t$ is caused by turbine $i$'s action at time $t - \Delta t$, where $\Delta t$ is the wake advection delay:
\begin{equation}
    c_j(t) = h\!\left(\text{wake}(a_i(t - \Delta t),\, v(t),\, \mathbf{x}_i,\, \mathbf{x}_j)\right)
    \label{eq:wake_delay}
\end{equation}
This is a \emph{state-dependent} cost: it depends on the wake state (position, intensity, turbulence), which is the result of past actions propagated through aerodynamic dynamics.
Modifying the current action $a_i(t)$ cannot change the wake that is already in transit.

\textbf{Safety Gym as a controlled diagnostic.}
To study this structure in a reproducible setting, we use Safety Gymnasium's SafetyPointGoal1-v0~\citep{ji2023safetygym}, which exhibits the same causal gap.
A point robot navigates to goals while avoiding circular hazard zones.
The cost is binary ($c=1$ when the agent enters a hazard zone) and depends on the agent's \emph{position}---the integral of past actions through momentum dynamics---not on the current action.

We tested five post-hoc correction mechanisms (Table~\ref{tab:safetygym}).
Action scaling and directional repulsion from lidar fail entirely---they modify action magnitude but cannot redirect the agent away from hazards it is already approaching.
A learned cost predictor $\hat{c}(s,a)$ (binary classifier, 90\% balanced accuracy) also fails via gradient correction, because the one-step cost prediction provides gradients that are too myopic.
The cost critic $Q_c(s,a)$, trained via Monte Carlo returns on 200 episodes ($\gamma_c = 0.99$, correlation with true returns $= 0.965$), provides the strongest results.
With clamped gradient steps or action sampling ($K=64$ candidates scored by $Q_c$), the cost critic achieves 20--36\% cost reduction while preserving $>$97\% reward.
At $B=40$ ($\sim$70\% of unconstrained cost), the $Q_c$ satisfies the budget at 97\% reward.
At $B=25$, the best configuration ($Q_c$ sample, $\gamma_c = 0.95$, $\eta=2$) reaches cost${}=29$---within 16\% of the budget.
At $B=10$, no mechanism satisfies the budget; the best achieves cost${}=33$ (3.3$\times$ over).

\textbf{Root cause: the action-to-cost coupling strength.}
The $Q_c$ achieves 96\%+ correlation with true cost returns but can only reduce cost by 20--36\%, because the action's influence on future cost is inherently limited: the agent's 2D action barely changes the trajectory determined by its 60D state (position, velocity, momentum).
We formalize the distinction:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Action-dependent costs}: $c_t = g(a_t, s_t)$ where $\nabla_a g \neq 0$. Post-hoc penalty composition works because the corrected action immediately reduces cost. \emph{Examples}: yaw angle $\to$ fatigue (simplified), velocity $\to$ energy cost.
    \item \textbf{State-dependent costs}: $c_t = g(s_t)$ where $s_t = f(s_{t-1}, a_{t-1})$. The gradient $\nabla_a c \approx 0$ because cost depends on accumulated state, not the current action. \emph{Examples}: position $\to$ hazard entry (Safety Gym), wake state $\to$ downstream fatigue (real wind farm), thermal state $\to$ bearing damage.
\end{itemize}

This distinction defines the \emph{scope condition} for post-hoc budget scheduling: the method is effective when the cost function provides useful gradients with respect to the current action.
The analogy between Safety Gym and wake-propagation fatigue is precise: in both cases, the cost at time $t$ is determined by a state variable (position, wake field) that is the integral of past actions through dynamics that the post-hoc correction cannot retroactively change.

\textbf{Implication.}
The simplified fatigue model ($|y/\bar{y}|^\beta \cdot (v/v_{\mathrm{ref}})^\gamma$) used in our wind farm experiments is action-dependent and thus amenable to post-hoc composition.
As fatigue models incorporate wake propagation, Miner's cumulative damage rule, or bearing thermal dynamics, they cross the boundary into state-dependent costs.
Bridging this gap is the key challenge for deploying budget-scheduled constraint enforcement on real wind farms.

\section{Toward Dynamics-Aware Budget Scheduling}
\label{sec:extensions}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The action-to-cost gap motivates a learned cost critic $Q_c$ that predicts long-horizon cost consequences.
We train $Q_c(s, a)$ offline from unconstrained policy rollouts and compose it with the urgency schedule via gradient correction or EBT energy addition.
The key question: does long-horizon cost prediction via $Q_c$ succeed where one-step prediction failed?

\textbf{Cost critic $Q_c$.}
We train a cost-value function on logged transitions from the unconstrained policy:
\begin{equation}
    Q_c(s, a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t'=t}^{T} \gamma_c^{t'-t} c(s_{t'}) \;\Big|\; s_t, a_t\right]
    \label{eq:cost_critic}
\end{equation}
using Monte Carlo returns $G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{T-t} \gamma_c^k c_{t+k}$ computed directly from episode data ($\gamma_c = 0.99$).
This avoids bootstrapping instability while capturing the full trajectory-level cost structure.
At deployment, the urgency schedule modulates $\nabla_a Q_c$:
\begin{equation}
    a_{\mathrm{corr}} = a_{\mathrm{actor}} - \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot \lambda(t) \cdot \alpha \cdot \nabla_a Q_c(s_t, a_t)
\end{equation}
The policy is never retrained; only $Q_c$ requires a one-time offline training phase from logged transitions.

\textbf{Wind farm result: $Q_c$ learns fatigue physics from data.}
On the 3-turbine wind farm, $Q_c$ trained on nonlinear fatigue cost achieves results comparable to the hand-crafted surrogate (Table~\ref{tab:qc_windfarm}).
The best configuration ($\eta=2$, gs=0.05) reduces cumulative fatigue by $5.7\times$ (from 54.6 to 9.5) while \emph{increasing} power to 114\% of unconstrained.
The $Q_c$ learns the same physical principle as the analytical surrogate---avoid aggressive yaw at high wind speeds---but discovers it from data rather than from a prescribed formula.
This is significant for deployment: the $Q_c$ can be trained from SCADA data without requiring an explicit fatigue model.

\textbf{Safety Gym result: $Q_c$ partially works on state-dependent costs.}
On SafetyPointGoal1-v0, the $Q_c$ achieves 96.5\% correlation with true cost returns.
With properly clamped gradient steps or action sampling ($K\!=\!64$ candidates scored by $Q_c$), it reduces cost by 20--36\% while preserving $>$97\% reward (Table~\ref{tab:safetygym}).
The budget is satisfied at $B=40$ ($\sim$70\% of unconstrained cost).
At tighter budgets, the $Q_c$ reduces cost but cannot achieve satisfaction---the best result at $B\!=\!25$ reaches cost${}=29$ (16\% over budget) and at $B\!=\!10$ reaches cost${}=40$ ($4\times$ over).

\textbf{When does $Q_c$ work?}
The contrast between domains reveals the operative condition: \emph{action-cost coupling strength}.
On the wind farm, yaw angle directly determines fatigue loading (gradient norm $\|\nabla_a Q_c\| \sim 1$--$10$)---changing the action changes the cost, yielding $5.7\times$ fatigue reduction.
On Safety Gym, the action has only a marginal effect on next-step position due to momentum---the cost is determined by trajectory history.
The $Q_c$ still provides useful gradients (reducing cost by 20--36\%), but the correction ceiling is lower because each action controls only a fraction of the future cost.
For real wind turbine fatigue with wake propagation, the coupling strength depends on the propagation delay relative to the control timescale: short delays (fast control, close spacing) preserve strong coupling; long delays (slow wind, large spacing) reduce it toward the Safety Gym regime.

\textbf{Future directions.}
For fundamentally state-dependent costs, approaches that reason about dynamics are needed:
control barrier functions that translate cumulative budgets into instantaneous action constraints via a QP at each step,
or model-predictive shielding that simulates $H$-step rollouts through a learned dynamics model.
These approaches add computational cost and model complexity but address the coupling problem at its root.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Constrained MDPs.}
The Lagrangian approach to CMDPs~\citep{altman1999constrained} learns a dual variable $\mu$ via primal-dual optimization.
PID-based methods~\citep{stooke2020responsive} improve stability.
Saute RL~\citep{sootla2022saute} augments the state with remaining budget but requires retraining.
Predictive Lagrangian Optimization~\citep{chen2025predictive} forecasts future multipliers but integrates with training.
CAPS~\citep{yao2024caps} switches between pre-trained policies for different constraint levels.
Brahmanage and Kumar~\citep{brahmanage2026bcr} condition on remaining budget via reachability analysis for offline safe RL.
TREBI~\citep{lin2023trebi} uses diffusion models with real-time budget constraints that can change at deployment, the closest prior work to our setting---but it requires an offline dataset and diffusion-specific training, while our method works with any pre-trained energy-based policy.
All of these methods require training or pre-training for each constraint class; our method requires none.

\textbf{Post-hoc constraint composition.}
Composable Energy Policies~\citep{urain2023composable} compose energy functions for robotic manipulation.
Constrained Diffusers~\citep{zhang2025constrained} add safety constraints via classifier guidance.
SafeDiffuser~\citep{xiao2023safediffuser} uses control barrier functions.
FISOR~\citep{zheng2024fisor} learns a feasibility classifier for diffusion-based safe RL.
PICNN~\citep{durkin2025picnn} corrects actions via input-convex networks.
All handle \emph{instantaneous} constraints; none address cumulative budgets that span an episode.

\textbf{Budget pacing.}
Online budget allocation is well-studied in advertising~\citep{balseiro2023budget}, where PID controllers and multiplicative updates pace daily spend.
Bandits with knapsacks~\citep{agrawal2014bandits} provide regret bounds for combinatorial budget problems.
Our work transfers this structure to RL deployment via energy composition.

\textbf{Optimal execution.}
Almgren and Chriss~\citep{almgren2001optimal} derive optimal liquidation trajectories from mean-variance objectives with endogenous market impact.
Recent work combines RL with execution~\citep{ning2024execution}.
Our work is inspired by the \emph{pacing logic} of optimal execution---the urgency ratio and TWAP baseline---but the analogy has clear limits: market impact (your trading affects future prices) has no analog in our setting, and the specific sinh trajectory does not transfer.
We adopt the conceptual framework (remaining inventory vs.\ remaining time) without claiming structural equivalence.

\section{Discussion: Competitive Landscape and Future Directions}
\label{sec:discussion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Honest assessment.}
The AC schedule's primary advantage is not raw performance at a single operating point but \emph{zero-shot adaptability}: the same $\eta$ parameter works across budget levels, domains, and deployment scenarios without per-task calibration.
A carefully tuned constant penalty can match AC's performance at any single budget level, but requires re-tuning when the budget changes.
The AC schedule handles budget changes by adjusting $u_t$ automatically---no search, no retraining, no additional evaluation budget.

In the wind farm domain, the AC schedule concentrates negative yaw into wake-aligned wind conditions and achieves near-unconstrained power.
The nonlinear fatigue surrogate provides the largest gains: a $6.8\times$ fatigue reduction demonstrates that the cost function matters more than the scheduling strategy.
In Safety Gymnasium, gradient correction is essential for spatial costs that action scaling cannot handle.

\textbf{When does the AC schedule win decisively?}
The advantage is largest when: (1)~the budget is tight relative to unconstrained behavior (forcing genuine allocation decisions), (2)~conditions vary within the episode (creating high-value and low-value windows), and (3)~the policy architecture supports energy composition or gradient correction (enabling smooth modulation rather than hard clipping).

\textbf{Future directions.}

\emph{Learned risk aversion.}
Currently $\eta$ is fixed for the entire episode.
A meta-learned $\eta(s_t)$ that adapts to the current state---high when the agent recognizes favorable conditions, low otherwise---could concentrate budget spending more precisely than the urgency ratio alone.
This would require a small auxiliary network trained offline on logged episodes.

\emph{Benefit prediction.}
The AC schedule reacts to budget state but does not predict future opportunities.
A lightweight forecasting model $\hat{b}(t+k \mid s_t)$ that estimates upcoming benefit (e.g., expected wind direction or upcoming terrain) could inform a lookahead urgency calculation, shifting from reactive to proactive budget management.

\emph{Multi-agent budget sharing.}
In the wind farm setting, turbines currently have independent budgets.
A shared farm-level budget with per-turbine allocation---analogous to portfolio optimization across assets---could exploit cross-turbine complementarities (e.g., spending T0's budget when T2 has surplus).

\emph{Tighter integration with policy training.}
The post-hoc paradigm trades optimality for flexibility.
A hybrid approach that \emph{trains} with a generic budget-awareness module (not tied to a specific budget level) and \emph{deploys} with the AC schedule for budget-specific adaptation could close the gap to retrained methods while preserving zero-shot generalization.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We introduced a penalty schedule inspired by pacing logic from optimal execution theory for enforcing cumulative budget constraints on pre-trained RL policies at deployment time.
The schedule adapts penalty strength based on the urgency ratio---remaining budget relative to remaining time---and composes post-hoc with pre-trained policies via per-agent energy addition for EBT actors or gradient-based action correction for standard actors.
Across two domains---wind farm yaw control with structural fatigue budgets and Safety Gymnasium navigation with hazard-exposure budgets---the method satisfies cumulative budgets while retaining high reward.

The method requires zero retraining but not zero tuning: the schedule parameter $\eta$ and guidance scale $\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}}$ require mild calibration via evaluation episodes, which is substantially cheaper than retraining but not free.
A single pre-trained policy serves arbitrary budget levels, per-agent heterogeneous constraints, and instant specification changes.
As RL systems move from research benchmarks to industrial applications where constraints emerge, evolve, and differ across installations, the ability to compose constraints post-hoc becomes increasingly important.

\textbf{Limitations.}
The Boltzmann response assumption (Proposition~\ref{prop:optimal}) is a motivating analysis, not an optimality guarantee.
The method works best when the base penalty scale is calibrated relative to the learned energy head.
Finite-sample constraint satisfaction relies on the exponential wall backstop rather than the smooth schedule alone.
The $Q_c$ extension requires a one-time offline training phase on logged transitions---substantially cheaper than policy retraining but not zero-cost.
For state-dependent costs (Safety Gym, realistic wake-propagation fatigue), the method achieves partial cost reduction (20--36\%) but cannot satisfy tight budgets due to weak action-to-cost coupling.
