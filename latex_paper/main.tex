\begin{abstract}
Deploying RL policies under cumulative operational constraints (maintenance budgets, duty-cycle limits) typically requires retraining for each constraint specification.
We propose a closed-form penalty weight based on the \emph{urgency ratio}---remaining budget divided by remaining time---that composes post-hoc with pre-trained policies via energy addition or gradient correction, requiring zero retraining.
A single parameter $\eta$ controls risk aversion; a single policy serves arbitrary budget levels instantly.
On wind farm yaw control, the schedule outperforms an oracle-tuned constant penalty (98.9\% vs.\ 96.7\% power retention) and a nonlinear fatigue surrogate achieves $6.8\times$ structural fatigue reduction.
We identify the \emph{action-to-cost coupling strength} as the scope condition: post-hoc correction works for action-dependent costs (wind farm) but partially fails for state-dependent costs (Safety Gym), where a learned cost critic $Q_c$ reduces cost by 20--36\% but cannot satisfy tight budgets.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Consider a wind farm operator who trains a reinforcement learning (RL) controller to maximize power production.
Six months after deployment, a bearing inspection reveals that one turbine needs restricted yaw travel: it may operate at negative yaw angles for at most 15 hours per year to limit structural fatigue.
Retraining the controller is expensive and disruptive.
The operator needs a way to impose this new cumulative constraint on the \emph{existing} policy, instantly and without retraining.

This scenario is ubiquitous: cumulative operational constraints arise whenever an agent must limit total time in a costly regime---wind turbines with load budgets~\citep{dijk2025load}, robots with joint-wear cycles, grid batteries with cycle-life limits, and advertisers with spending caps~\citep{balseiro2023budget}.
Unlike instantaneous constraints, these are \emph{lifetime budgets}: the agent must decide \emph{when} to spend its limited capacity for risky actions.

Inspired by optimal trade execution~\citep{almgren2001optimal}, where a trader paces liquidation based on remaining inventory vs.\ remaining time, we propose an \emph{urgency ratio} that adapts constraint strength to budget state.

Existing approaches do not solve this:
\textbf{CMDP methods}~\citep{altman1999constrained, stooke2020responsive, sootla2022saute} require retraining for each budget specification.
\textbf{Post-hoc composition}~\citep{urain2023composable, zhang2025constrained, xiao2023safediffuser} handles only instantaneous constraints, not cumulative budgets spanning an episode.

We fill this gap with three contributions:
(1)~a \textbf{closed-form penalty schedule} $w(u) = \exp(\eta(1/u - 1))$ derived from budget-balanced allocation, parameterized by a single risk-aversion scalar $\eta$;
(2)~\textbf{post-hoc composition} with pre-trained policies via energy addition (EBT actors) or gradient correction (MLP actors), enabling zero-retraining budget enforcement;
(3)~\textbf{empirical validation} on wind farm yaw control ($6.8\times$ fatigue reduction, 98.9\% power) and Safety Gymnasium (diagnostic of state-dependent cost limitations).

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Constrained MDPs and Lagrangian Relaxation}

A CMDP~\citep{altman1999constrained} augments the MDP objective with cost constraints: $\max_\pi \mathbb{E}_\pi[\sum_t r_t]$ s.t.\ $\mathbb{E}_\pi[\sum_t c_t^i] \leq d^i$ for each agent $i$, where $c_t = c(s_t, a_t)$ is the per-step cost and $d$ is the cost limit (following standard CMDP notation~\citep{ray2019safetygym, achiam2017cpo}).
The standard Lagrangian approach learns a dual variable $\mu$ via primal-dual optimization~\citep{stooke2020responsive}, requiring retraining whenever $d$ changes.

\subsection{Energy-Based RL and Post-Hoc Composition}

Energy-Based Transformer (EBT) actors~\citep{gladstone2025ebt} generate actions by gradient descent on a learned energy $E_\phi^i(\mathbf{e}^i, a^i)$.
External energy terms compose per-agent before aggregation:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\mathrm{total}}(a) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\!\left[E_\phi^i(\mathbf{e}^i, a^i) + \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot E_{\mathrm{constraint}}^i(a^i)\right]
    \label{eq:composition}
\end{equation}
enabling post-hoc constraints without modifying $E_\phi$~\citep{urain2023composable, zhang2025constrained}.
We extend this from instantaneous constraints to \emph{cumulative budget constraints} via a time-varying energy weight.

\section{Optimal Budget Scheduling}
\label{sec:theory}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

An agent allocates cost limit $d$ over $T$ steps.
Per-step cost $c_t := c(s_t, a_t) \geq 0$; cumulative $C_t := \sum_{s=0}^{t-1} c_s$ (in wind fatigue, Miner's-rule damage).
The \emph{urgency ratio} captures the budget--time state:
\begin{equation}
    u_t = \frac{\rho_t}{\tau_t}, \qquad
    \rho_t = \frac{d - C_t}{d}, \qquad
    \tau_t = \frac{T - t}{T}
    \label{eq:urgency}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_t$ is the remaining budget fraction and $\tau_t$ the remaining time fraction.
When $u > 1$, the agent has underspent; when $u < 1$, overspent.
Per-agent budgets $d^i$ yield $u_t^i$ computed independently per agent.

\begin{proposition}[Motivating analysis under idealized Boltzmann response]
\label{prop:optimal}
Consider an idealized policy that responds to penalty weight $\lambda$ via a Boltzmann model:
$P(\text{risky} \mid \lambda) = P_0 \exp(-\alpha \lambda)$,
where $P_0$ is the base risky-action probability and $\alpha > 0$ is the policy's sensitivity.
Under this model, the penalty weight that maintains budget balance has a multiplicative structure determined by the urgency ratio:
\begin{equation}
    w^*(u) = \frac{1}{u}
    \label{eq:optimal}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Budget balance requires $(T-t) \cdot P(\text{risky} \mid \lambda) = d - C_t$.
Substituting the Boltzmann model, solving for $\lambda$, and computing the multiplicative weight relative to the TWAP baseline ($u=1$) yields $w = \tau/\rho = 1/u$.
The full derivation is given in Appendix~\ref{app:proof}.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Practical schedule.}
Since $1/u \to \infty$ as $u \to 0$ (budget depleted) and the Boltzmann response is an idealization that does not hold for real policy architectures, we use a parametric family that preserves the key structural properties:
\begin{equation}
    w(u) = \exp\!\left(\eta \cdot \left(\frac{1}{u} - 1\right)\right)
    \label{eq:practical}
\end{equation}

The parameter $\eta \geq 0$ controls risk aversion:
$\eta = 0$ recovers the constant-penalty baseline (no adaptation to budget state);
$\eta > 0$ increases the penalty when overspent ($u < 1$) and relaxes it when underspent ($u > 1$).

\textbf{Hard wall backstop.}
A steep exponential barrier activates when budget drops below 5\%:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{\mathrm{wall}}(t) = \exp\!\left(k \cdot \mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(1 - \frac{\rho_t}{0.05}\right)\right)
\end{equation}
The combined weight is $\lambda(t) = \min(w(u_t) \cdot \lambda_{\mathrm{wall}}(t),\; \lambda_{\max})$, clamped at $\lambda_{\max} = 10^6$.

\textbf{Approximation quality.}
The practical schedule $\exp(\eta(1/u-1))$ matches $u^{-\eta}$ within 5\% for $u \in [0.7, 1.3]$; the approximation quality is detailed in Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:approx}.
Ablation results (Table~\ref{tab:hard_guard}) show that the smooth schedule alone is sufficient in practice.

\textbf{Penalty landscape.}
The TWAP diagonal ($\rho = \tau$, where $u=1$) separates surplus ($\lambda < 1$) from deficit ($\lambda > 1$); higher $\eta$ produces steeper gradients near this boundary (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:contours}).

\textbf{Note on the Boltzmann assumption.}
The Boltzmann response is a motivating idealization; the derivation identifies $u$ as the natural state variable and constrains the schedule to be monotone decreasing in $u$, but the specific form $\exp(\eta(1/u-1))$ is a principled parametric choice rather than an optimality claim.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The method has three components that compose independently:
(1)~the \emph{budget schedule} $\lambda(t)$ from Section~\ref{sec:theory}, which determines \emph{how strictly} to enforce the constraint at each moment;
(2)~the \emph{cost function} $c(s, a)$, which defines \emph{what} is costly;
and (3)~the \emph{application mechanism}, which determines \emph{how} the penalty modifies actions.
Any cost function composes with any mechanism and the same $\lambda(t)$ schedule.

\subsection{Cost Functions}
\label{sec:cost}

The simplest cost is a binary indicator $c(s, a) = \mathbf{1}[\text{yaw} < 0]$, ignoring severity.
A nonlinear surrogate captures the physics:
$c(s, a) = (|a|/\bar{a})^p \cdot (v(s)/v_{\mathrm{ref}})^q$,
\label{eq:nonlinear_cost}
where $p\!=\!2$ (quadratic yaw-fatigue) and $q\!=\!3$ (cubic wind-speed amplification) are physically motivated~\citep{dijk2025load}.
All cost functions compose with the same $\lambda(t)$ schedule; swapping the cost requires no retraining.

\subsection{Application Mechanisms}
\label{sec:composition}

\textbf{Energy composition (EBT actors).}
The cost composes directly with the policy energy before action optimization:
$E_{\mathrm{total}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i [E_\phi^i + \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \lambda^i(t) P(c(s, a^i))]$,
where $P(x) = \exp(k \cdot \mathrm{ReLU}(x)) - 1$.

\textbf{Gradient correction (any actor).}
\label{sec:gradient}
For standard actors, we correct the output via:
\begin{equation}
    a_{\mathrm{corrected}} = a_{\mathrm{actor}} - \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot \lambda(t) \cdot \alpha \cdot \nabla_a\, c(s, a_{\mathrm{actor}})
    \label{eq:gradient_correction}
\end{equation}
iterated for $K$ steps.
The gradient handles nonlinear costs that action scaling cannot: $\nabla_a c$ points away from costly regions, not just toward lower magnitude.

\subsection{Algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Post-Hoc Budget-Constrained Inference}
\label{alg:budget}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Trained actor, cost limit $d^i$ per agent, horizon $T$, risk aversion $\eta$, cost $c(s,a)$
\STATE $C_0^i \leftarrow 0$ for all agents $i$
\FOR{$t = 0, \ldots, T-1$}
    \STATE Observe $s_t$; compute $u_t^i = \frac{(d^i - C_t)/d^i}{(T-t)/T}$, $\lambda^i(t) = \min(\exp(\eta(1/u_t^i - 1)) \cdot \lambda_{\mathrm{wall}}^i,\; \lambda_{\max})$
    \STATE Generate $a_t$: \hfill\COMMENT{choose one mechanism}
    \STATE \quad \textit{EBT:} gradient descent on $E_\phi + \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot \lambda^i(t) \cdot P(c(s_t, a))$
    \STATE \quad \textit{MLP:} $a \leftarrow a_{\mathrm{actor}} - \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot \lambda^i(t) \cdot \alpha \cdot \nabla_a c(s_t, a)$ for $K$ steps
    \STATE Execute $a_t$, observe $r_t$; $c_t^i = c^i(s_t, a_t)$
    \STATE $C_{t+1}^i \leftarrow C_t + c_t^i$
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The $\lambda$ computation adds 4.7~ms overhead (19.8\%) on the wind farm EBT actor.
The parameters $\eta \in [2, 5]$ and $\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \in [0.05, 0.5]$ require mild calibration via evaluation episodes---cheaper than retraining but not zero-cost.
Unlike the Lagrangian dual $\mu$ in CMDPs (learned via gradient ascent, requiring retraining), $\lambda^i(t)$ is computed in closed form, enabling instant adaptation to new budget specifications.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We evaluate the budget scheduling mechanism across two domains with different risky-action semantics.

\subsection{Domain 1: Wind Farm Yaw Control}

\textbf{Environment.}
WindGym~\citep{windgym} with PyWake backend.
We test on two layouts: a 3-turbine row and a 9-turbine $3\!\times\!3$ grid.
Wind speed sampled from 10--14~m/s, wind direction from 225--315$^\circ$, providing natural temporal variation in wake conditions.

\textbf{Risky action.}
Negative yaw angles ($a^i < 0$) steer the rotor against the conventional direction, deflecting wakes to increase downstream power but imposing structural loads on the yawed turbine~\citep{dijk2025load}.

\textbf{Budget.}
$B$ = total timesteps at negative yaw per turbine per episode.

\textbf{Agent.}
Energy-Based Transformer (EBT) actor with Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), trained unconstrained for 100k steps.
The budget surrogate composes post-hoc via per-turbine energy addition at inference.

\subsection{Domain 2: Safety Gymnasium Navigation}

SafetyPointGoal1-v0~\citep{ji2023safetygym}: a point robot navigates to goals while avoiding hazard zones ($c\!=\!1$ on entry).
Unlike the wind farm, cost is \emph{spatial}---it depends on position, not action direction.
SAC with MLP actor, trained unconstrained for 200k steps.
This domain serves as a diagnostic for state-dependent costs where the action-to-cost coupling is weak.

\subsection{Baselines and Ablations}

Baselines: unconstrained (upper bound), hard clip (lower bound), constant penalty ($\eta\!=\!0$, static Lagrangian), and our AC schedule ($\eta \in \{2, 5\}$).
Additionally, we compare against an oracle-tuned constant penalty found via 15-step bisection search.
Ablations on the wind farm test the hard guard, schedule form ($1/u^\eta$ vs.\ $\exp$), and budget flexibility (Appendix).

\subsection{Ablations}

The AC schedule alone keeps all turbines within budget ($[11, 11, 11]$ at $d\!=\!15$) without the hard guard; the constant penalty overshoots at $[29, 51, 58]$.
The $1/u^{\eta}$ and $\exp(\eta(1/u\!-\!1))$ schedules perform comparably.

Full ablation tables are in the Appendix.

\subsection{Scope: Action-Dependent vs.\ State-Dependent Costs}
\label{sec:state_dependent}

The method succeeds when cost depends on the current action ($\nabla_a c \neq 0$).
Realistic fatigue involves \emph{state-dependent} costs---wake propagation delays decouple upstream yaw from downstream loading~\citep{dijk2025load}.
Safety Gym~\citep{ji2023safetygym} exhibits the same structure: position-dependent cost with momentum dynamics.

To bridge this gap, we train a cost critic $Q_c(s,a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[\sum \gamma_c^k c_{t+k}]$ offline (MC returns, $\gamma_c\!=\!0.99$, correlation 0.965) and modulate $\nabla_a Q_c$ via the urgency schedule.
On the wind farm (strong coupling), $Q_c$ achieves $5.7\times$ fatigue reduction; on Safety Gym (weak coupling), 20--36\% cost reduction at $>$97\% reward, satisfying budget at $d\!=\!40$ but not tighter (Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:qc_windfarm},~\ref{tab:safetygym}).
The \emph{coupling strength}---$\|\nabla_a Q_c\|$ relative to $\|\nabla_s Q_c\|$---determines the correction ceiling.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Constrained MDPs.}
Lagrangian CMDP methods~\citep{altman1999constrained, achiam2017cpo, stooke2020responsive, chen2025predictive} learn dual variables during training.
SAUTE~\citep{sootla2022saute} augments state with remaining budget $\rho_t$ (same variable we use) but still retrains the policy, so each new budget requires a new policy.
TREBI~\citep{lin2023trebi} handles real-time budget changes via diffusion models but requires offline data and diffusion-specific training.
Our method instead freezes the policy and uses a closed-form $\lambda(t)$ schedule at inference---no retraining for any budget.

\textbf{Post-hoc constraint composition.}
Energy composition~\citep{urain2023composable}, classifier guidance~\citep{zhang2025constrained}, control barrier functions~\citep{xiao2023safediffuser}, and input-convex corrections~\citep{durkin2025picnn} handle \emph{instantaneous} constraints; none address cumulative budgets spanning an episode.

\textbf{Budget pacing and optimal execution.}
Budget allocation in advertising~\citep{balseiro2023budget} and bandits with knapsacks~\citep{agrawal2014bandits} provide pacing strategies.
Our work adopts the urgency-ratio framework from optimal execution~\citep{almgren2001optimal}---remaining inventory vs.\ remaining time---without claiming structural equivalence to market-impact models.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We introduced a closed-form urgency-ratio schedule for enforcing cumulative budget constraints on pre-trained RL policies without retraining.
On wind farm yaw control, a nonlinear fatigue surrogate achieves $6.8\times$ fatigue reduction while retaining 98\% of power; a learned cost critic $Q_c$ discovers the same physics from data ($5.7\times$ reduction).
On Safety Gym, $Q_c$ achieves 20--36\% cost reduction at $>$97\% reward, with the action-to-cost coupling strength determining the ceiling.
The method requires zero policy retraining but mild calibration of $\eta$ and $\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}}$; the $Q_c$ extension requires offline training on logged transitions.
For state-dependent costs where action-to-cost coupling is weak (momentum-dominated dynamics, wake propagation delays), future work on control barrier functions or model-predictive shielding may close the gap.

\subsection{Full Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:optimal}}
\label{app:proof}

\begin{proof}
Budget balance requires that the expected number of risky actions over the remaining horizon equals the remaining budget:
\begin{equation}
    (T - t) \cdot P(\text{risky} \mid \lambda) = d - C_t
    \label{eq:balance}
\end{equation}
Substituting the Boltzmann model: $(T-t) \cdot P_0 \exp(-\alpha \lambda) = d - C_t$.
Solving: $\lambda = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\!\frac{(T-t)\,P_0}{d - C_t}$.
At the TWAP baseline ($u = 1$, where $\rho = \tau$, \ie, $(d - C_t)/d = (T-t)/T$), the budget balance gives $\lambda_0 = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\!\frac{T P_0}{d}$.
The multiplicative weight relative to the TWAP penalty is:
\[
    w = \exp\!\big(\alpha(\lambda - \lambda_0)\big) = \frac{(T-t)\,P_0}{d - C_t} \cdot \frac{d}{T\,P_0} = \frac{(T-t)/T}{(d - C_t)/d} = \frac{\tau}{\rho} = \frac{1}{u}
\]
\end{proof}
