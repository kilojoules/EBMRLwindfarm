\begin{abstract}
Deploying RL policies under cumulative operational constraints (maintenance budgets, duty-cycle limits) typically requires retraining for each new constraint specification.
We propose a closed-form penalty weight based on the \emph{urgency ratio}---remaining budget divided by remaining time---that composes post-hoc with pre-trained policies via energy addition or gradient correction, requiring zero policy retraining.
A single risk-aversion scalar $\eta$ governs the schedule; a single policy serves arbitrary budget levels instantly.
We further identify a dimensionless quantity---the \emph{action-to-cost coupling strength} $\kappa = \mathbb{E}\|\nabla_a Q_c\|/\mathbb{E}\|\nabla_s Q_c\|$---that predicts the method's scope \emph{a priori}.
On two domains with a $36\times$ gap in $\kappa$, the predictor is validated in both directions: wind farm yaw control ($\kappa\!\approx\!0.72$) retains 98\% of unconstrained power while reducing fatigue $1.6\times$ vs.\ unconstrained (and $6.8\times$ vs.\ a naive binary-cost penalty) and satisfies the budget across 4 training seeds ($95.9 \pm 3.1\%$ of unconstrained power); Safety Gymnasium navigation ($\kappa\!\approx\!0.02$) predictably fails at tight budgets across 5 seeds, exposing where post-hoc correction is and is not appropriate.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Consider a wind farm operator whose deployed controller suddenly faces a new maintenance constraint: turbine~2's yaw may be negative for at most 15~hours per year.
Retraining is expensive.
The operator needs to impose this cumulative constraint on the \emph{existing} policy, instantly and without touching the policy weights.
This scenario is ubiquitous---cumulative operational limits arise whenever an agent must bound total time in a costly regime: wind turbines with load budgets~\citep{dijk2025load}, robots with joint-wear cycles, grid batteries with cycle-life limits, advertisers with spending caps~\citep{balseiro2023budget}.
Unlike instantaneous constraints, these are \emph{lifetime budgets}: the agent must decide \emph{when} to spend its limited capacity for risky actions.

\textbf{Existing approaches do not solve this.}
CMDP methods~\citep{altman1999constrained, stooke2020responsive, sootla2022saute} retrain the policy per budget.
Post-hoc composition~\citep{urain2023composable, zhang2025constrained, xiao2023safediffuser} handles instantaneous but not cumulative constraints.
Inspired by optimal trade execution~\citep{almgren2001optimal}---a trader paces liquidation against remaining inventory vs.\ remaining time---we propose an \emph{urgency-ratio} schedule $\lambda(t)$ that adapts constraint strength to budget state and composes with any pre-trained actor.

\textbf{The key question is not whether the schedule works, but \emph{when}.}
Post-hoc correction modifies the action at inference; its leverage is limited by how much the future cost depends on the current action.
We formalize this via the coupling strength
\begin{equation}
\kappa := \mathbb{E}_{(s,a)\sim\pi}\,\|\nabla_a Q_c(s,a)\|_2 \,/\, \mathbb{E}_{(s,a)\sim\pi}\,\|\nabla_s Q_c(s,a)\|_2 ,
\label{eq:kappa}
\end{equation}
where $Q_c(s,a)$ is a learned cost critic (see~\S\ref{sec:scope}).
Large $\kappa$: action has strong leverage over future cost (post-hoc correction works).
Small $\kappa$: cost is state-dominated, the correction gradient is weak (post-hoc correction fails).
Figure~\ref{fig:kappa_diag} visualizes this contrast on the two domains we study.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_kappa_diagnostic.png}
    \caption{Coupling-strength diagnostic $\kappa = \mathbb{E}\|\nabla_a Q_c\|/\mathbb{E}\|\nabla_s Q_c\|$ predicts whether post-hoc budget correction works. \textbf{Contributions.}
(1)~A \emph{closed-form} budget-scheduling penalty $w(u)=\exp(\eta(1/u-1))$ derived from budget-balanced allocation (\S\ref{sec:theory}), with a single risk-aversion parameter $\eta$.
(2)~\emph{Post-hoc composition} with pre-trained policies via energy addition (EBT actors) or gradient correction (MLP actors), requiring zero policy retraining (\S\ref{sec:method}).
(3)~A \emph{coupling-strength scope predictor} $\kappa$ (\S\ref{sec:scope}) that forecasts success/failure before deployment.
(4)~\emph{Empirical validation} on two domains with $36\times$ difference in $\kappa$: wind farm ($1.6\times$ fatigue reduction vs.\ unconstrained at 98\% power, $95.9 \pm 3.1\%$ retention across 4 seeds; nonlinear cost avoids the fatigue inflation induced by a naive binary penalty) and Safety Gymnasium (predicted failure across 5 seeds; \S\ref{sec:results}).

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Constrained MDPs.}
A CMDP~\citep{altman1999constrained} adds cost-return constraints to the MDP objective:
$\max_\pi \mathbb{E}_\pi[\sum_t r_t]$ subject to $\mathbb{E}_\pi[\sum_t c_t^i] \leq d^i$,
where $c_t = c(s_t,a_t)$ and $d^i$ is the cost limit (standard CMDP notation~\citep{ray2019safetygym, achiam2017cpo}).
Lagrangian methods~\citep{stooke2020responsive} learn a dual variable $\mu$ during training; changing $d$ requires a new policy.

\textbf{Energy-based actors and composition.}
Energy-Based Transformer (EBT) actors~\citep{gladstone2025ebt} generate actions by gradient descent on a learned energy.
External terms compose per-agent before aggregation:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\mathrm{total}}(a) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\!\left[E_\phi^i(\mathbf{e}^i,a^i) + \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}} \cdot E_{\mathrm{constraint}}^i(a^i)\right]
    \label{eq:composition}
\end{equation}
enabling post-hoc constraints without modifying $E_\phi$~\citep{urain2023composable,zhang2025constrained}.
We extend this from instantaneous constraints to \emph{cumulative budgets} via a time-varying weight.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Urgency-Ratio Budget Schedule}
\label{sec:theory}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

An agent allocates budget $d$ over horizon $T$.
Per-step cost $c_t := c(s_t,a_t) \geq 0$; cumulative $C_t := \sum_{s=0}^{t-1} c_s$ (in wind fatigue: Miner's-rule damage~\citep{dijk2025load}).
The \emph{urgency ratio} captures the budget--time state:
\begin{equation}
    u_t = \frac{\rho_t}{\tau_t}, \qquad
    \rho_t = \frac{d - C_t}{d}, \qquad
    \tau_t = \frac{T - t}{T}
    \label{eq:urgency}
\end{equation}
$\rho_t$ is the remaining budget fraction, $\tau_t$ the remaining time fraction.
$u > 1$ means the agent has underspent; $u < 1$ means overspent.
Per-agent budgets $d^i$ yield $u_t^i$ computed independently per agent.

\begin{proposition}[Idealized-Boltzmann motivation]
\label{prop:optimal}
If the policy responds to penalty weight $\lambda$ via
$P(\mathrm{risky}\mid\lambda)=P_0\exp(-\alpha\lambda)$, then the penalty weight maintaining budget balance takes the multiplicative form
\begin{equation}
    w^*(u) = 1/u .
    \label{eq:optimal}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\textit{Proof sketch.} Budget balance $(T-t)P(\mathrm{risky}\mid\lambda)=d-C_t$ with the Boltzmann substitution gives $\lambda=\frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\frac{(T-t)P_0}{d-C_t}$.
The weight relative to TWAP ($u=1$) reduces to $\tau/\rho = 1/u$.
Full derivation in Appendix~\ref{app:proof}.\hfill$\square$

\textbf{Practical schedule.}
Since $1/u\!\to\!\infty$ as $u\!\to\!0$ and the Boltzmann response is an idealization, we use a parametric family preserving the key structural properties:
\begin{equation}
    w(u) = \exp\!\Big(\eta\cdot\big(\tfrac{1}{u}-1\big)\Big),
    \qquad \eta \geq 0 .
    \label{eq:practical}
\end{equation}
$\eta=0$ recovers a constant-penalty baseline; $\eta>0$ tightens under deficit and relaxes under surplus.
$w(u)$ and $u^{-\eta}$ agree within 5\% for $u\in[0.7,1.3]$ (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:approx}).

\textbf{Hard-wall backstop.}
A steep barrier activates when budget drops below 5\%:
$\lambda_{\mathrm{wall}}(t) = \exp(k\cdot\mathrm{ReLU}(1-\rho_t/0.05))$.
The combined weight is $\lambda(t) = \min(w(u_t)\,\lambda_{\mathrm{wall}}(t),\lambda_{\max})$ with $\lambda_{\max}=10^6$.
Ablations (Appendix~\ref{app:ablations}) show that the smooth schedule alone is sufficient in practice.

\textbf{Note.}
The Boltzmann derivation is a motivating idealization that identifies $u$ as the natural state variable and constrains $w$ to be monotone-decreasing in $u$; $\exp(\eta(1/u-1))$ is a principled parametric choice rather than an optimality claim.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The method has three components that compose independently:
(1)~the budget schedule $\lambda(t)$ from~\S\ref{sec:theory} (\emph{how strictly});
(2)~the cost function $c(s,a)$ (\emph{what is costly});
(3)~the application mechanism (\emph{how} the penalty modifies actions).

\textbf{Cost functions.}
The simplest cost is a binary indicator $c(s,a)=\mathbf{1}[\mathrm{yaw}<0]$.
A nonlinear fatigue surrogate captures the physics:
$c(s,a) = (|a|/\bar{a})^p(v(s)/v_{\mathrm{ref}})^q$, with $p\!=\!2$, $q\!=\!3$ (quadratic yaw-fatigue, cubic wind-speed amplification~\citep{dijk2025load}).

\textbf{Application mechanisms.}
(i)~\emph{Energy composition} for EBT actors: $E_{\mathrm{total}}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_i[E_\phi^i+\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}}\lambda^i(t)P(c(s,a^i))]$ with $P(x)=\exp(k\cdot\mathrm{ReLU}(x))-1$.
(ii)~\emph{Gradient correction} for any actor:
\begin{equation}
    a \leftarrow a - \lambda_{\mathrm{gs}}\cdot\lambda(t)\cdot\alpha\cdot\nabla_a\,c(s,a), \quad K \text{ steps}.
    \label{eq:gradient_correction}
\end{equation}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Post-Hoc Budget-Constrained Inference}
\label{alg:budget}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Trained actor, limit $d^i$, horizon $T$, risk aversion $\eta$, cost $c(s,a)$
\STATE $C_0^i \leftarrow 0$ for all agents $i$
\FOR{$t = 0, \ldots, T-1$}
    \STATE Observe $s_t$; $u_t^i = \frac{(d^i-C_t^i)/d^i}{(T-t)/T}$; $\lambda^i(t)=\min(\exp(\eta(1/u_t^i-1))\cdot\lambda_{\mathrm{wall}}^i,\lambda_{\max})$
    \STATE Generate $a_t$ (choose mechanism): \emph{EBT:} gradient descent on $E_\phi+\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}}\lambda^i(t)P(c)$;\quad\emph{MLP:} Eq.~\ref{eq:gradient_correction}
    \STATE Execute $a_t$, observe $r_t$, $c_t^i = c^i(s_t,a_t)$; $C_{t+1}^i \leftarrow C_t^i + c_t^i$
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The schedule $\lambda$ adds 4.7~ms (19.8\%) overhead on the wind farm EBT actor.
$\eta\!\in\![2,5]$ and $\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}}\!\in\![0.05,0.5]$ require mild calibration (a few evaluation episodes), cheaper than retraining but not zero-cost.
Unlike the Lagrangian dual $\mu$ (learned via gradient ascent~\citep{stooke2020responsive}), $\lambda^i(t)$ is computed in closed form, enabling instant adaptation to new budget specifications.

\section{Scope: Coupling Strength as a Predictor}
\label{sec:scope}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The schedule $\lambda(t)$ modulates a correction signal; its effectiveness is capped by how much the future cost depends on the current action.
When the cost function is directly action-dependent ($\nabla_a c \neq 0$), $\lambda$ multiplies a useful gradient.
When the cost is state-dependent---as with position-conditioned hazards or wake-propagation delays---the immediate $\nabla_a c$ is weak, and the correction must instead flow through a learned cost critic $Q_c(s,a)=\mathbb{E}_\pi[\sum \gamma_c^k c_{t+k}]$.
We train $Q_c$ offline on frozen-policy rollouts (MC returns, $\gamma_c\!=\!0.99$, correlation $0.965$; Figure~\ref{fig:qc_pipeline}) and use $\nabla_a Q_c$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:gradient_correction}.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \resizebox{0.9\linewidth}{!}{\input{figures/fig_qc_pipeline.tex}}
    \caption{$Q_c$ pipeline. \textbf{A.}~Collect transitions from frozen policy rollouts. \textbf{B.}~Fit $Q_c$ on Monte Carlo cost returns. \textbf{C.}~Deploy: actor proposes action, $Q_c$ gradient corrects it $K$ times per step. Policy weights unchanged throughout.}
    \label{fig:qc_pipeline}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Coupling strength $\kappa$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:kappa}).}
Large $\kappa$: the current action has strong leverage over future cost (expect post-hoc correction to work).
Small $\kappa$: cost is state-dominated (expect tight budgets to fail).
We measure $\kappa$ on 2000 on-policy samples per domain and report it in Table~\ref{tab:coupling}.
The $36\times$ gap between wind farm and Safety Gym \emph{forecasts} the experimental results (\S\ref{sec:results}) before we deploy the method.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering

\label{tab:coupling}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule

\midrule

\textbf{Operational test.}
Given a new domain, compute $\kappa$ on logged rollouts in under a minute (Appendix~\ref{app:coupling_protocol}).
Our two observed values differ by two orders of magnitude and predict qualitatively opposite outcomes; a precise threshold requires more domains and is left to future work.
Pragmatically: if $\kappa$ for a new domain is comparable to our wind-farm value ($\sim\!1$), post-hoc correction is plausible; if it is comparable to our Safety-Gym value ($\sim\!10^{-2}$), state-space methods (MPC, control-barrier-function shielding) are indicated.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:results}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We test the two predictions made by $\kappa$ (\S\ref{sec:scope}): success on wind farm yaw control ($\kappa\!\approx\!0.72$) and failure on Safety Gymnasium at tight budgets ($\kappa\!\approx\!0.02$).
Both domains use the same schedule $\lambda(t)$, the same $Q_c$ training recipe, and the same correction mechanism.

\subsection{Wind Farm: Strong Coupling, Budget Satisfied}

\textbf{Environment.}
WindGym~\citep{windgym} with PyWake backend; 3-turbine row and 9-turbine $3\!\times\!3$ grid; wind speed 10--14 m/s; direction 225--315$^\circ$.
Negative yaw steers rotors against the conventional direction, deflecting wakes to raise downstream power but imposing structural loads on the yawed turbine~\citep{dijk2025load}.
Budget $d$ = total timesteps at negative yaw per turbine per episode.
Actor: EBT trained unconstrained with SAC for 100k steps; the budget surrogate composes post-hoc via per-turbine energy addition at inference.

\textbf{AC schedule vs.\ oracle constant (single seed, 50 episodes).}
Table~\ref{tab:main_results} compares the AC schedule ($\eta\!=\!5$, gs$\!=\!0.1$) against an oracle-tuned constant penalty (15-step bisection search) at $d\!=\!15$: AC achieves 98.9\% power retention without per-budget tuning; the oracle achieves 96.7\% and requires re-tuning for every new $d$.

\textbf{Multi-seed AC (4 training seeds).}
Averaged across 4 independently trained EBT actors using the \emph{same} $(\eta,\text{gs})$ configuration, AC delivers $95.9 \pm 3.1\%$ of unconstrained power with neg-yaw $[11,12,12]$; all 4 seeds satisfy the budget.

\textbf{Nonlinear fatigue cost matters.}
Table~\ref{tab:nonlinear} compares binary vs.\ nonlinear costs.
Binary AC ($\eta\!=\!2$) avoids negative yaw entirely but accumulates fatigue $86.2$---$4.2\times$ \emph{worse} than unconstrained ($20.3$)---because it pushes the agent to large positive yaw.

The lesson is methodological: the cost function choice dominates; a well-chosen nonlinear cost prevents the binary-penalty failure mode.

\subsection{Safety Gymnasium: Weak Coupling, Predicted Failure}
\label{sec:sg_results}

\textbf{Environment.}
SafetyPointGoal1-v0~\citep{ji2023safetygym}: a point robot navigates to goals while avoiding circular hazards ($c\!=\!1$ on hazard entry).
Action space is 2D (thrust, turn); cost is spatial, driven by position relative to hazards.
Actor: SAC/MLP trained unconstrained for 200k steps, 5 independent seeds.

\textbf{Multi-seed result (Table~\ref{tab:safetygym}).}
At all tested budgets $d\in\{10,25,40\}$ and both no-correction and gradient-correction modes, cost remains near the unconstrained baseline ($53.1 \pm 3.0$ at $d\!=\!10$ with correction; $53.6 \pm 2.1$ unconstrained).
Every seed violates every tested budget.
This is exactly what $\kappa\!\approx\!0.02$ predicts: the correction gradient $\nabla_a Q_c$ is $\sim\!50\times$ smaller than $\nabla_s Q_c$, so state drift---not the action---dominates cost accumulation.

\textbf{Pessimistic $Q_c$ does not rescue the method (Appendix~\ref{app:uncertainty}).}
We trained an ensemble of 10 $Q_c$ critics (5 seeds $\times$ twin heads) and tested pessimistic correction $\nabla_a(\bar{Q}+\kappa\sigma)$ with $\kappa\in\{0,1,4\}$ and $d\in\{10,25,40\}$.
No monotonic $\kappa^*$ exists: $\kappa\!=\!0$ wins or ties at every budget (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:pessimism}).
Ensemble disagreement \emph{does} reliably flag failure states (Spearman $\rho\!=\!+0.195$ vs.\ cost-ahead; stronger than $\|\Delta a\|$ at $+0.064$), but gating the correction on uncertainty does not exploit the signal when the underlying $\nabla_a Q_c$ is dominated by noise.

\textbf{Compute note.}
Each wind-farm training seed costs approximately 3~GPU-hours (LUMI/MI250X, 100k SAC steps on a 3-turbine row); each Safety-Gym seed costs approximately 1.5~GPU-hours (200k SAC steps).
$Q_c$ training adds $\sim\!20$~minutes per domain (offline MC fit on logged transitions).
Because our effect sizes are large---every Safety-Gym seed violates every tested budget; the wind-farm schedule beats an oracle on retention---the $n\!=\!4$/$n\!=\!5$ seed counts are adequate, although more seeds would tighten the wind-farm standard deviation ($3.1\%$).

\textbf{Takeaway: the predictor works in both directions.}
The coupling strength $\kappa$ forecasts both the wind-farm success and the Safety-Gym failure from a single sub-minute measurement on logged rollouts.
When $\kappa$ is small, action-space correction is structurally the wrong tool---not a matter of hyperparameter tuning.
We recommend switching to state-space methods (MPC, control-barrier-function shielding) for such domains.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Constrained MDPs.}
Lagrangian methods~\citep{altman1999constrained,achiam2017cpo,stooke2020responsive,chen2025predictive} learn dual variables during training.
SAUTE~\citep{sootla2022saute} augments state with remaining budget $\rho_t$ (same variable we use) but still retrains per budget.
TREBI~\citep{lin2023trebi} handles real-time budget changes via diffusion models but requires diffusion-specific training.
We freeze the policy and use a closed-form $\lambda(t)$ at inference.

\textbf{Post-hoc constraint composition.}
Energy composition~\citep{urain2023composable}, classifier guidance~\citep{zhang2025constrained}, control barrier functions~\citep{xiao2023safediffuser}, and input-convex corrections~\citep{durkin2025picnn} handle instantaneous constraints.
None address cumulative budgets spanning an episode.

\textbf{Budget pacing.}
Budget allocation in advertising~\citep{balseiro2023budget} and bandits with knapsacks~\citep{agrawal2014bandits} share the allocation-across-time structure.
We adopt the urgency-ratio framework from optimal execution~\citep{almgren2001optimal} without claiming structural equivalence to market-impact models.

\textbf{Coupling-strength scope conditions.}
We are not aware of prior scalar predictors specifically targeting the scope of post-hoc action-space correction; critic-gradient magnitudes have been analysed in offline-RL distributional work, controllability Grammians in classical control, and input-to-state stability in nonlinear systems, all of which address related but distinct questions.
Our $\kappa$ uses the gradient \emph{ratio} $\|\nabla_a Q_c\|/\|\nabla_s Q_c\|$ as an \emph{a-priori} predictor of action-level correction leverage.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We introduced a closed-form urgency-ratio schedule for enforcing cumulative budget constraints on pre-trained RL policies without retraining, and a coupling-strength predictor $\kappa$ that forecasts its scope.
Wind farm yaw control ($\kappa\!\approx\!0.72$) reduces fatigue $1.6\times$ vs.\ unconstrained at 98\% power (avoiding the $4.2\times$ fatigue inflation caused by a naive binary-cost AC) and satisfies budget across 4 seeds.
Safety Gymnasium ($\kappa\!\approx\!0.02$) predictably fails at tight budgets across 5 seeds: the cost is state-dominated, and no amount of action-space correction can close the gap.
The scope predictor works in both directions on a single measurable quantity, turning what would otherwise be a deployment surprise into a pre-deployment decision.
\textbf{Limitations.}
The schedule requires mild calibration of $\eta$ and $\lambda_{\mathrm{gs}}$; $Q_c$ requires a one-time offline training phase (cheaper than retraining, not zero-cost).
For weakly coupled domains, post-hoc correction is the wrong tool---future work should investigate state-space shielding~\citep{xiao2023safediffuser} or model-predictive methods that can leverage the dominant $\nabla_s Q_c$ signal.

\subsection{Full Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:optimal}}
\label{app:proof}

\begin{proof}
Budget balance requires that the expected number of risky actions over the remaining horizon equals the remaining budget:
\begin{equation}
    (T-t)\cdot P(\mathrm{risky}\mid\lambda) = d - C_t .
    \label{eq:balance}
\end{equation}
Substituting the Boltzmann model $P(\mathrm{risky}\mid\lambda)=P_0\exp(-\alpha\lambda)$ and solving:
$\lambda = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\!\frac{(T-t)P_0}{d-C_t}$.
At the TWAP baseline ($u=1$, \ie\ $(d-C_t)/d=(T-t)/T$), $\lambda_0=\frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\!\frac{TP_0}{d}$.
The multiplicative weight relative to TWAP is:
\[
    w = \exp(\alpha(\lambda-\lambda_0)) = \frac{(T-t)P_0}{d-C_t}\cdot\frac{d}{TP_0} = \frac{(T-t)/T}{(d-C_t)/d} = \frac{\tau}{\rho} = \frac{1}{u}.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Pessimistic $Q_c$ and Uncertainty Diagnostic}
\label{app:uncertainty}

\paragraph{Failure-state predictors.}
We asked whether two predictors distinguish $Q_c$ failure: (i)~ensemble disagreement $\sigma(Q_c)$ across 5 cost critics, and (ii)~action-space deviation $\|\Delta a\|$ between actor proposal and corrected action.
Across 20k Safety Gym state samples at $d\!=\!10$, ensemble disagreement correlates with cost-over-next-$H$ at Spearman $\rho\!=\!+0.195$; $\|\Delta a\|$ achieves $\rho\!=\!+0.064$ (Figure~\ref{fig:discriminator}).

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/fig_discriminator.pdf}
    \caption{Predictors of $Q_c$ failure. Ensemble disagreement $\sigma(Q_c)$ tracks realized cost $\sim\!3\times$ more strongly than $\|\Delta a\|$.}
    \label{fig:discriminator}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Pessimism null result.}
Motivated by the discriminator, we tested pessimistic correction $\nabla_a(\bar{Q}+\kappa\sigma)$ over $\kappa\in\{0,1,4\}\times d\in\{10,25,40\}$ (50 episodes each, seeds pinned).
$\kappa=0$ (single critic) wins or ties at every budget (Table~\ref{tab:pessimism}).
Ensemble uncertainty correctly flags failure states, but the pessimism-modulated gradient does not exploit the signal when the underlying $\|\nabla_a Q_c\|$ is dominated by noise in the weak-coupling regime.

\subsection{Coupling Strength Measurement Protocol}
\label{app:coupling_protocol}

For each domain, we roll out 20 episodes under the frozen policy, subsample 2000 $(s,a)$ pairs uniformly, and compute
$\|\nabla_a Q_c(s,a)\|_2$ and $\|\nabla_s Q_c(s,a)\|_2$ at each sample via automatic differentiation.
The reported $\kappa$ is the ratio of sample means; standard errors in Table~\ref{tab:coupling} are computed by nonparametric bootstrap with 1000 resamples.
The protocol requires the $Q_c$ checkpoint and a few hundred logged transitions---no environment rollouts beyond what is already needed to fit $Q_c$.
